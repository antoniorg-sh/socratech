#+DEK:       Standard RAG is a lookup into a frozen index. A Hebbian layer lets the weights between chunks shift as you use them, so the same question tomorrow pulls in a different neighborhood than today. A note on building one, and on what a memory actually is.
#+FEATURED:  t

Standard retrieval-augmented generation has a particular shape. You
break a corpus into chunks, you embed each chunk, you store the
embeddings in a vector index. A query arrives, you embed it, you
pull the top-k nearest neighbors, you hand them to the model.
Tomorrow the same query gives you the same chunks. The day after
that, the same. The index does not learn. It is a frozen photograph
of its corpus at the moment it was built.

The brain, the thing the technology is named after, does not work
this way.

** A note from 1949

Donald Hebb, in 1949, wrote down what would become the most famous
sentence in neuroscience: when an axon of cell A is near enough to
excite cell B and repeatedly takes part in firing it, some growth
process occurs such that A's efficiency in firing B is increased.
The usual translation is "cells that fire together wire together,"
and while it loses the hedges, it keeps the load-bearing idea.
Co-activation strengthens a connection. Over time, what the network
fires in response to what depends on what it has fired together
with.

This is a rule you can drop into a retrieval system.

** The cascade

The version I have been building works in four steps. A query arrives
and runs a TF-IDF pass first, which is fast and vocabulary-bounded
and tells you what chunks share words with the question. Then a
cosine similarity pass on local embeddings (Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B,
running in LM Studio on my laptop), which tells you what chunks
share meaning with the question even if the words differ. Then comes
a Hebbian neighborhood pass, which does something the first two
cannot: it looks at what chunks have been co-retrieved with this
chunk before, and pulls them in too. Finally, a content relevance
gate: if nothing scores high enough, the system returns nothing
rather than fabricating connections.

Each layer catches something the one before it misses. The words-
layer catches exact matches. The meaning-layer catches paraphrases.
The Hebbian layer catches the things that are related only because
they have answered a question together before. The gate keeps the
system honest when the corpus genuinely has nothing to say.

** How the graph moves

When retrieval returns a set of chunks, and the reader (a human, or
a downstream model acting as judge) marks that answer useful, the
edges between those chunks get strengthened. When the answer is
judged bad, or when a chunk has not been part of a useful retrieval
in a long time, its edges decay. This is the standard machinery of
Hebbian learning (potentiation and decay) applied to the index
rather than to a population of neurons.

What you get, after a few hundred queries, is a graph that has
quietly reorganized itself around the questions it has been asked.
Chunks that kept proving useful together live close to each other in
retrieval space, even when their raw embedding similarity would not
have suggested it. Chunks that no one ever reaches for slowly fall
out of the picture.

The implementation is smaller than you would expect. A sparse
matrix of edge weights keyed by chunk ID, a simple rule on every
retrieval (bump the chunks that helped, decay the rest by a
constant factor), a re-ranker on the read path that adds the
accumulated co-activation score to the embedding score with a
small coefficient. That is most of it. The rest is bookkeeping
about what counted as a good answer.

** Why this is interesting

Two reasons, and they are both philosophical before they are
technical.

The first is that a frozen index is a strange artifact. It is the
only part of a knowledge system that does not get better with use.
The model gets better. The prompts get better. The product iterates.
But the retrieval underneath stays the shape it had on day one.
That asymmetry shows up in the output. Systems that do not learn
from use feel the same tomorrow as they did today, and users
eventually notice, even if they cannot name what they are noticing.

The second is that notes, and knowledge, are not static objects.
A library is not an archive. A library is a pattern of paths that
get worn into the carpet by the people who walk them. Some books
get picked up often; others gather dust. The books that come
together in the same reader's afternoon end up in the same mental
neighborhood, regardless of how the shelves are arranged. A
retrieval layer that mirrors that behavior is closer to what a
memory actually is. The closer the tool gets to how the brain
actually treats the world, the less work the brain has to do to
treat it.

** What it is not

It is not a replacement for the embedding layer. The embedder stays
frozen. The Hebbian layer sits on top as a re-ranker that remembers
what the query has been paired with before. This is the cheap
part. No one has to fine-tune a model to get this; anyone with an
embedding index and a small sparse graph can have it.

It is also not magic. If the feedback signal is bad (a user who
clicks thumbs-up on everything, a reader-model that judges its own
answers uncritically, a noisy eval suite that rewards confident
wrongness), the weights drift toward noise. The quality of the
learning is the quality of the signal that drives it. This is the
part that is harder than it looks, and the part I keep returning
to.

** Where this is going

I use the word "brain" carefully, because everyone calls everything
a brain now. What I mean is narrow. A Hebbian memory layer is an
index that responds to being used. It is one ingredient in the
larger kind of thing people have in mind when they say "agent with
memory," and a small one. But it is the ingredient that closes the
loop between what the system has seen and what the system will see
next.

The more interesting claim is that this loop, once closed,
compounds. The more an agent uses the memory, the more specific the
memory becomes to that agent. That is the same property that makes
a person's notebook unlike anybody else's: the shape of what has
been written down gets rearranged by the shape of what has been
looked up. A Hebbian layer is one attempt to give a machine the
same grace.

The code that runs this lives at =~/hebbian-memory/=. I am still
deciding what the right benchmark for it is. Precision at five is
the obvious one; the harder question is whether the improvement
from the Hebbian layer is large enough to be felt in use, or only
large enough to be detected by a microbenchmark. The first is the
only kind of improvement that matters. The second is the kind you
can publish.

I want both, eventually. For now I want the first.
